Appendicitis — Pathophysiology

Incidence
• Most common acute abdominal surgical emergency
• Peak: 2nd-3rd decades (20-30 years)
• Rare in children (wide opening, well drained)
• Rare in elderly (lumen mostly obliterated)
Pathophysiology
Obstruction is the major cause
Causes of obstruction:
• Fecalith/appendicolith (most common)
• Lymphoid hyperplasia
• Vegetable matter or seeds
• Parasites, neoplasm
Progression:
1) Obstruction → bacterial overgrowth
2) Mucus secretion → ↑ intraluminal pressure
3) Visceral pain → periumbilical
4) Lymphatic/venous impairment → mucosal ischemia
5) Localized inflammation → peritoneal irritation
6) RLQ pain (somatic pain from parietal peritoneum)
Timeline:
• Perforation typically after ≥48 hours
• Followed by localized or generalized peritonitis
Course (Untreated)
Persistent obstruction:
• Gangrene → perforation
• At tip or site of impacted fecalith
• High perforation rate: children <5, elderly
• → Generalized or localized peritonitis
Slower process:
• Body defense: wall off by intestine/omentum
• Within 3-5 days: appendicular mass formed
• Perforation within mass → appendicular abscess
Portal pyemia (pylephlebitis):
• Rare complication
• Suppurative thrombophlebitis of portal vein
• → Liver abscess
Bacteriology
• Polymicrobial (similar to colon)
• E. coli, Streptococcus viridans
• Bacteroides, Pseudomonas species
