Peritonitis — Classification

Bacterial Peritonitis
GI Sources:
• Viscus perforation (ulcer, appendix)
• Transmural bacterial translocation
Organisms:
• E. coli, Streptococci, Bacteroides
• Clostridium, Klebsiella
Non-GI Sources:
• Female genital tract
• Exogenous (surgery, dialysis)
• Hematogenous (rare)
Organisms:
• Chlamydia, N. gonorrhoeae
• TB, Fungal (immunocompromised)
Chemical Peritonitis
• Initial stage of gastric/duodenal perforation
• Bile or pancreatic leakage
• Sterile for up to several hours
• Secondarily infected by transmural spread
Primary Peritonitis
• Diffuse bacterial infection WITHOUT obvious source
Organisms:
• Pneumococcus (most common)
• Hemolytic Streptococcus (children)
• E. coli, Klebsiella (ascites + cirrhosis)
Localized vs Diffuse
Localized (favoring factors):
• Peritoneal compartments (subphrenic, pelvic, supracolic/infracolic)
• Adhesions (fibrin)
• Retarded peristalsis
• Greater omentum barrier
Diffuse (favoring factors):
• Speed of contamination
• Peristalsis stimulation (food, purgatives)
• Virulent organisms
• Deficient omentum (children)
• Deficient resistance (drugs, AIDS, old age)
