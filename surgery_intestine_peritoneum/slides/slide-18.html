Abdominal & Pelvic Abscesses

Pelvic Abscess
Most common site of abscess
Etiology:
• Appendicitis (pelvic position)
• Fallopian tube infection
• Anastomotic leakage
• Sequel to diffuse peritonitis
Features:
• Pelvic pain, diarrhea, mucus in stools
• PR: bulging anterior rectal wall (softly cystic when ripe)
Tx:
• US/CT confirmation
• May burst into rectum → rapid recovery
• Vaginal drainage (posterior fornix)
• Rectal drainage
• CT-guided percutaneous drainage
• Laparotomy almost never necessary
Subphrenic Abscess Sites
Left Subphrenic:
• Causes: gastric, pancreatic tail, splenic flexure surgery
Left Subhepatic/Lesser Sac:
• Causes: complicated pancreatitis, perforated gastric ulcer
Right Subphrenic:
• Causes: perforating cholecystitis, duodenal ulcer, gastrectomy, appendicitis
Right Subhepatic (Morison's Pouch):
• Deepest space, most common subphrenic abscess
• Causes: appendicitis, cholecystitis, duodenal ulcer, upper abdominal surgery
"Pus somewhere, pus nowhere, pus under the diaphragm"
Clinical Features & Tx
Features:
• Swinging pyrexia
• Post-surgical improvement → deterioration after days/weeks
• Non-specific symptoms
Investigation:
• CT scan (investigation of choice)
Treatment:
• CT-guided percutaneous drainage
• Appropriate antibiotics
Key Points:
• Gravity determines abscess location
• Sitting vs supine position matters
• Complications of intra-abdominal sepsis
• Must identify and treat the source
