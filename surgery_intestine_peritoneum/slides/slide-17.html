Peritonitis — Clinical & Treatment

Clinical Features
Localized:
• Visceral inflammation → abdominal pain
• Peritoneal inflammation → worsening pain
• Localized guarding, rebound tenderness
• Rigidity (involuntary constant contraction)
• Subphrenic → shoulder-tip (C5) pain
• Pelvic → rectal/vaginal tenderness
Diffuse (Early):
• Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, malaise
• Severe pain, worse with movement
• Tenderness + generalized guarding
• Infrequent → absent bowel sounds
Diffuse (Late):
• Circulatory failure, thready pulse
• Cold clammy extremities
• Hippocratic facies
• Unconsciousness → septic shock/MODS
• Generalized rigidity + distension
Investigations
Labs:
• U&Es, FBC (WCC), Serum amylase
X-ray:
• Erect CXR: free air under diaphragm
• Lateral decubitus if too ill
• Supine: dilated loops (ileus)
US:
• Pelvic peritonitis in women
• RUQ peritonism
CT:
• Increasingly used to identify cause
Treatment (General):
1. Correct fluid/electrolyte loss
2. Urinary catheter ± NG decompression
3. Broad-spectrum antibiotics
4. Analgesia (epidural may be excellent)
• Nurse sitting up
• Early mobilization → prevent DVT, PE, basal collapse
Specific Treatment
Surgical (when diagnosis amenable):
• Operate within hours when fit
• Deal with specific cause
• Explore whole peritoneal cavity
• Mop dry, remove all exudate
• Lavage with large volume saline
• Drainage
Non-surgical preferred for:
• Pancreatitis
• Salpingitis
• Primary streptococcal/pneumococcal peritonitis
Timing:
• Patient must be fit for anesthesia
• Resuscitation must restore physiology
