Section 7 • Diarrheal Diseases

Whipple's Disease

Definition & Epidemiology

  • Multisystemic disease caused by Tropheryma whipplei (gram+ bacterium).
  • Affects small intestine (malabsorption), joints, CNS, CVS (major cause of culture-negative endocarditis).
  • Extremely rare. More common in white middle-aged/older males.

Route of Transmission

Unknown but occupational exposure to animals highly documented.

Pathogenesis & Histology

  • Immune system incorporates organisms into tissue macrophages (visible on light microscopy infiltrating tissues).
  • Malabsorption: disruption of normal villous function due to infiltration of lamina propria.
  • Histology: Lamina propria full of periodic acid-Schiff (PAS)-positive macrophages (from intestinal, synovial, CNS, or valve lesions).

Investigations

  • Lab: Sudan stain of stool, serum carotene, LFT. 72h fecal fat determination (definitive for malabsorption).
  • PCR: rpoB gene segment of T. whipplei — highly sensitive.
  • Serologic tests available but shouldn't be used.
  • Imaging: CT/small bowel series suggest malabsorption (not specific). Brain MRI for CNS disease.

Complications

  • 30-40% relapse (more in CNS disease)
  • Antibiotic reaction/allergy

Prognosis

  • Untreated: mortality ~100% after 1 year
  • Treated for full year: prognosis good, ~70% remission

Treatment

  • Induction: IV Ceftriaxone 2g OD or Penicillin G 2M units q4h ×14 days
  • Maintenance: Oral double-strength TMP-SMX bid ×1 year
  • Other: Streptomycin, Penicillin V, Amoxycillin, Chloramphenicol
