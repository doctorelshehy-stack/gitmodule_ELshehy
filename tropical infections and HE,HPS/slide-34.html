Section 6 • Toxoplasmosis

Organism, Transmission & Clinical

Causative Organism

Intracellular parasite Toxoplasma gondii. Cats and their prey are the definitive hosts.

Route of Transmission

  • Primary: Ingestion of tissue cysts from soil, food or water contaminated by cat feces
  • ~⅓ of women contracting T. gondii during pregnancy transmit to fetus
  • 65% risk of transmission if maternal infection in 3rd trimester

Clinical Manifestations

  • Most infections: asymptomatic or trivial
  • Symptomatic: Lymphadenopathy (head & neck), fever, myalgia, malaise
  • Severe: hepatitis, pneumonia, myocarditis, choroidoretinitis
  • Lymphadenopathy & fatigue can persist for months

Congenital Toxoplasmosis

  • May be asymptomatic or produce serious disease
  • Microcephaly, hydrocephalus, encephalitis, convulsions, mental retardation
  • Choroidoretinitis common (may be only feature)

Immunocompromised (HIV)

Risk of serious infection. Usually reactivation of latent disease.

Life Cycle (from diagram)

Definitive host (cats) → oocysts in feces → sporulated oocysts → ingested by intermediate hosts → tissue cysts → uncooked meat ingested → humans. Also: Tachyzoites transmitted through placenta → infected fetus. Oocysts in food/water/soil.
