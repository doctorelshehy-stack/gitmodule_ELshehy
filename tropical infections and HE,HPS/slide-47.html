Section 7 • Diarrheal Diseases

Intestinal Tuberculosis

Epidemiology

  • 1-3% of TB worldwide. Less common than LN, GU, bone/joint, miliary, meningeal TB.
  • High risk: HIV (most important — up to 50% of TB in HIV presents as extrapulmonary), malignancies (lymphoma), glucocorticoids/anti-TNF agents.

Causative Organism & Route

  • M. tuberculosis — most cases. M. bovis — from contaminated dairy.
  • Route: Direct penetration of intestinal mucosa by swallowed organisms (sputum or contaminated milk/food). Or direct extension from adjacent organs or miliary spread.
  • Chest films often normal at diagnosis.

Pathology

  • Ileum & cecum — most common (75%). Both sides of ileocecal valve involved → valve incompetence (helps distinguish from Crohn's).
  • 3 types: (1) Ulcerative (60%) — multiple superficial lesions. (2) Hypertrophic (10%) — scarring, fibrosis, mass (mimics carcinoma). (3) Ulcerohypertrophic (30%) — mixed.

Clinical Presentations

  • Acute, chronic, or acute-on-chronic (resembles Crohn's).
  • TB toxemia: night fever, sweating, weight loss.
  • Most common: non-specific chronic abdominal pain (80-90%).
  • Diarrhea/constipation, blood in stool.
  • Palpable abdominal mass (25-50%) — ileum most common site.
  • Perianal disease with fistula described.

Investigations

  • Lab: Mild anemia, normal WBC. High ESR/CRP, +ve tuberculin test, +ve Quantiferon Gold, PCR in tissue/ascites.
  • Imaging: Abd US (bowel thickening, lymphadenopathy, ascites, omental thickening). CT abdomen (mass/bowel thickening).
  • Barium study (from image): Pulled up cecum, obtuse ileocecal angle/straightening (Goose neck), Steirlin sign (barium hurrying), Fleischner sign/inverted umbrella (narrow ileum + thickened valve), napkin lesions, chicken intestine (hypersegmentation), mega ileum.
  • Biopsy: Characteristic tuberculoma.

Complications & Treatment

Complications: Hemorrhage, fistula, perforation (uncommon, can occur during treatment), intestinal obstruction (more common, from segmental stenosis), malabsorption (from proximal SIBO).
Treatment: Anti-tuberculous drugs. Surgical intervention may be required to relieve obstruction despite drug therapy.
