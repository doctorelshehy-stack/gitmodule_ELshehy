Section 5 • Malaria

Clinical Manifestations

General Features

  • Incubation: P. vivax/ovale/falciparum: 10-14 days. P. malariae: 18 days to 6 weeks
  • Initial non-specific: headache, fatigue, myalgias → fever
  • Febrile paroxysms at regular intervals → suggest P. vivax/ovale
  • Splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, mild anemia, jaundice
  • With repeated exposure → resistance to high-level parasitemia and disease (not infection)
  • Hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell, ovalocytosis, thalassemia) and G6PD deficiency protect against death

Severe Falciparum Malaria (requires ≥1 of:)

  • CNS: Impaired consciousness/coma, repeated convulsions
  • Severe normocytic anemia, renal failure, pulmonary edema/ARDS
  • Circulatory shock, DIC, spontaneous bleeding, acidosis
  • Hemoglobinuria, jaundice, parasitemia >5%
  • Cerebral malaria: Diffuse symmetric encephalopathy without focal signs. Coma → mortality ~20%
  • Pregnancy: Unusually severe. Premature labor, fetal distress, stillbirth, low birth weight
  • Tropical splenomegaly: Chronic complication → massive splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, abnormal immune response

From organ system diagram: Neurological (coma, hypoglycemia, seizures, cranial nerve palsies, opisthotonus, disconjugate gaze, Roth's spots). Respiratory (pulmonary edema, secondary bacterial pneumonia). Cardiovascular (shock, algid malaria, dysrhythmias with quinine). Renal (acute renal failure, blackwater fever). Abdomen (jaundice, tender liver, splenomegaly). Blood (parasitemia, anemia, thrombocytopenia, coagulopathy).
