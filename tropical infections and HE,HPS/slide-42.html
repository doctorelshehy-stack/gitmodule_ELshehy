Section 7 • Diarrheal Diseases

Acute Diarrhea — HUS, C. difficile, Cholera, Giardia

Hemolytic-Uremic Syndrome (HUS)

  • Triad: acute renal failure + microangiopathic hemolytic anemia + thrombocytopenia.
  • 10-15% of EHEC infections, 5-7 days after symptom onset.
  • Most likely at extremes of age, high WBC count. May be induced by antibiotics.
  • Treatment: Dialysis & plasma exchange.

6. C. difficile Infection

  • History of antibiotics in last 6 weeks (most common: clindamycin).
  • Toxins A+B → severe diarrhea, pseudomembranous colitis, toxic megacolon.
  • High risk: hospitalized, elderly, previous attacks, IBD.
  • Dx: Stool exam for cytotoxin, stool culture.
  • Tx: Metronidazole 400mg q8h ×10d. Vancomycin 125mg q6h ×1wk.

7. Cholera

  • Travel to endemic area. Contaminated food/water.
  • Massive non-bloody watery diarrhea "rice water", no odor.
  • Dehydration occurs quickly. Vaccine available but short-lived.
  • Tx: Hydration + Tetracycline, ampicillin, azithromycin, TMP-SMX, fluoroquinolones.

8. Giardiasis

  • One of most common causes of diarrhea.
  • Transmitted: water, food, person-to-person.
  • Watery diarrhea, foul-smelling soft/greasy stools. Abdominal cramps, bloating.
  • Usually clears within 6 weeks. May have recurrent episodes or post-infectious intestinal problems.
  • Tx: Tinidazole (Fasigyn) 2g single dose. Metronidazole (Flagyl) 250mg q8h ×5d.

HIV-associated Diarrhea: Direct HIV effects (CD4>200: viral/IBS). Medications (nelfinavir, didanosine, antibiotics). CD4<200: C. difficile, salmonellosis. CD4<100: Cryptosporidium, Microsporidia, Isospora belli. CD4<50: Mycobacterium avium, CMV.
