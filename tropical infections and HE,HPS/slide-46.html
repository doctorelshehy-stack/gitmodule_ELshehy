Section 7 • Diarrheal Diseases

Tropical Sprue

Definition & Epidemiology

  • Chronic diarrhea, malaise, weight loss, malabsorption of carbohydrates, fats, vitamin B12, and folate.
  • Affects indigenous residents of or travelers to tropical areas.
  • Occurs in narrow band north/south of equator to 30° latitude.
  • Common: India, Pakistan, Burma, Indonesia, Borneo, Malaysia, Singapore, Vietnam.
  • Uncommon: Africa, China, Middle East.
  • Affects visitors staying >1 month. Seldom in visits <2 weeks.
  • Incidence declining due to: improved hygiene, widespread antibiotics, better celiac serology.

Historical Background

"Sprue" from Dutch "sprouw" (1669). Tropical sprue coined by Sir Patrick Manson (1880).

Pathogenesis & Causative Agents

  • Strong presumption: caused by enteric infection, perhaps in nutritionally predisposed individuals.
  • Supporting facts: often initiated by acute diarrheal episode; epidemic/seasonal nature; responds to antibiotics ± nutritional supplements.
  • Bacteria: Enterotoxigenic E. coli & K. pneumoniae. Visitors: impairment of expulsion of enterotoxigenic coliforms after acute diarrhea.
  • Protozoa: Cryptosporidium parvum, Isospora belli.
  • Viruses, fungi, yeasts also considered. No single agent fully reproduces all features.

Histology

  • Villus blunting + lamina propria infiltration (more marked than bacterial overgrowth)
  • Delayed small bowel transit

Treatment & Prognosis

  • Treatment: Broad-spectrum antibiotic (e.g., tetracycline) + vitamin B12 + folic acid for 6 months.
  • Prognosis: Usually improves, mucosa reverts toward normal. If no improvement → presumptive celiac sprue.
