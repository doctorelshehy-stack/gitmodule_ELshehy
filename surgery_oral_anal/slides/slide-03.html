Sialadenitis — Overview

Definition
• Inflammation and enlargement of salivary glands
• Commonly affects parotid and submandibular glands
Risk Factors (↓ Salivary Flow)
• Dehydration, malnutrition, immunosuppression
• Medications (diuretics, antihistamines)
• Oral cavity surgery, duct anomalies
• Retrograde bacterial colonization
Causes
Infectious: Staph aureus, Pseudomonas, Mumps
Obstructive: Stones (parotid/submandibular)
Autoimmune: SLE, RA, Sjogren, Scleroderma
Chronic Sclerosing (Kuttner's): IgG4-related, submandibular
Clinical Features
• Unilateral/bilateral painful swelling
• Dysphagia
• Fever, headache, malaise
• Displacement of earlobe (parotid)
• Pus from ducts (spontaneous or manipulated)
• Dry mouth/eye (Sjogren)
Obstructive sialadenitis:
• Abrupt episodic pain around mealtimes
• Persists 2-3 hours after eating
Investigations
• Plain X-ray (panorama/occlusal) for stones
• Digital subtraction sialography
• US (parenchyma, duct, stones)
• MRI, Scintigraphy (⁹⁹ᵐ pertechnetate)
• Culture & sensitivity of pus
• ANA, RF (autoimmune exclusion)
• FNAC (sclerosing sialadenitis)
Treatment
• Broad-spectrum antibiotics (acute bacterial)
• Stone removal (obstructive)
• Salivary substitute + treat underlying disease (autoimmune)
• Hydration + pain relief (sclerosing)
