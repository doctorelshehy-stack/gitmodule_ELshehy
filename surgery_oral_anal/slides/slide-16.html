Rectal Prolapse

Definition & Types
• Mucosal or full-thickness rectal tissue protrudes through anus
Types:
1) Full-thickness (most common)
2) Mucosal prolapse only
3) Internal intussusception (does not pass anus)
Pathophysiology
• Sliding hernia through pelvic floor defect
• Starts as circumferential intussusception 6-8 cm above anus
• Progresses with straining to full prolapse
Etiology & Anatomic Features
• Pregnancy, pelvic floor dysfunction
• Diarrhea, BPH, perineal trauma
• Weak anal sphincter + levator dysfunction
• Deep anterior Douglas pouch
• Poor posterior rectal fixation, long mesentery
Children:
• Vertical rectal orientation, mobile sigmoid
• Weak pelvic floor, poor mucosal fixation
• Parasitic infestation, decreased sacral curvature
Clinical Features
• Mass protruding during defecation
• Retracts on standing or manual reduction
• Pain, anal bleeding
• Fecal incontinence
• Difficulty with defecation (internal intussusception)
Examination (squatting):
• Protruding rectal mucosa
• Thick concentric mucosal ring
• Sulcus between anal canal and rectum
• Solitary rectal ulcer (10-25%)
• Decreased sphincter tone
Investigations
• Stool analysis (children: exclude parasites)
• Video defecography (internal prolapse, intussusception)
• Barium enema / colonoscopy (exclude other lesions)
• Anorectal manometry (↓ resting pressure)
Treatment
Non-operative:
• Gentle digital reduction
• Bulking agents, stool softeners, suppositories
• Biofeedback (paradoxical pelvic floor contraction)
Surgical:
• Abdominal: Laparoscopic rectopexy, anterior resection
• Perineal: Anal encirclement (Thiersch wire), Delorme mucosal sleeve resection, hemorrhoidectomy
