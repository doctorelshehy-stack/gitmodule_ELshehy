HPS — Imaging Techniques

Imaging studies for HPS aim to demonstrate intrapulmonary vasodilation (IPVD) and exclude underlying cardiopulmonary disease:

1) Chest X-Ray
To exclude significant pulmonary disease or pleural effusion. In HPS, CXR may be normal or show nonspecific findings.
2) High-Resolution CT Chest
Useful in selected cases. Can show dilated peripheral pulmonary vessels and an increased pulmonary artery-to-bronchus ratio (indicating vasodilation). Also excludes interstitial lung disease and emphysema.
3) Contrast Echocardiography
The gold standard for detecting IPVDs. Agitated saline or indocyanine green is injected IV to produce microbubbles. In the presence of a right-to-left shunt, microbubbles appear in the right atrium/ventricle and then in the left atrium/ventricle. Timing of appearance differentiates intracardiac (within 3 beats) vs intrapulmonary (3-6 beats) shunting.
4) Nuclear Scanning
Technetium-99m-labeled macroaggregated albumin (MAA) scanning. Normally, labeled albumin particles are trapped in the pulmonary capillary bed. In HPS, particles pass through dilated pulmonary vessels and are detected in the brain/kidneys, confirming right-to-left shunting. Allows quantification of shunt fraction.
5) Pulmonary Angiography
Invasive diagnostic tool with limited experience in HPS. Can be useful to exclude conditions that mimic HPS such as pulmonary embolization. May demonstrate two patterns: Type 1 (diffuse, fine spider-like vessels) and Type 2 (focal, discrete arteriovenous communications).
