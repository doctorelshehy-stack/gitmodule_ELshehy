HPS — Treatment

Conservative

Oxygen can be used in all patients.

Long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT) improves symptoms and quality of life. Supplemental oxygen corrects hypoxemia and relieves dyspnea. Target SaO₂ > 90%.

Medical

Not effective.

Multiple pharmacological agents have been studied including: somatostatin analogues (octreotide), nitric oxide inhibitors (methylene blue), almitrine, indomethacin, and antibiotics. None have shown consistent benefit in randomized trials. There is currently no FDA-approved medical therapy for HPS.

Surgical

Liver transplantation in patients with PaO₂ > 55-60 mmHg and shunt fraction < 20%.

Liver transplantation (LT) is the only definitive treatment for HPS. HPS is a MELD exception qualifying for additional MELD points. Post-transplant, HPS resolves in >80% of patients over 6-12 months. Severe HPS (PaO₂ <50 mmHg) carries higher post-transplant mortality but still improves in many survivors.

Treatment Summary:

  • All patients with HPS should receive supplemental oxygen as needed to maintain SaO₂ ≥ 90%
  • Patients with severe HPS (PaO₂ <60 mmHg) should be evaluated for liver transplantation
  • HPS severity improves post-LT in most patients, but severe hypoxemia (PaO₂ <50 mmHg) increases perioperative risk
  • No effective pharmacological therapy currently exists — management is supportive
  • Embolization of intrapulmonary shunts may benefit select patients with Type 2 (focal) HPS
