HE — Etiology

The etiology of HE is primarily due to accumulation of toxins in the serum due to liver dysfunction. The major factors leading to HE are:

  • Reduction in hepatic function and hepatic mass
  • Development of porto-systemic collaterals which lead to circulatory bypass of the liver
  • Failure of the liver to detoxify gut-derived neurotoxins

Key Toxins Implicated in HE Pathogenesis:

Ammonia — The toxin most frequently implicated in pathogenesis of HE. Produced in the gut from protein breakdown and nitrogenous waste. Normally detoxified by the liver via the urea cycle.

GABA — The principal neuro-inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA is increased in the CSF of patients with encephalopathy, contributing to neural inhibition.

Endogenous Benzodiazepines — Increased levels of endogenous benzodiazepine-like compounds found in CNS, potentiate GABA-A receptor activity.

Other Toxins — Manganese deposition in basal ganglia, oxygen free radicals, circulating opioid peptides, and nitric oxide all contribute to CNS dysfunction.

These compounds gain access to systemic circulation due to decreased hepatic function or portal-systemic shunts. Once in brain tissue, they produce alterations of neurotransmission that affect consciousness, behavior, motor dysfunction and extrapyramidal symptoms.
