Summary & Key Takeaways

Hepatic Encephalopathy

  • Neuropsychiatric syndrome due to liver dysfunction and porto-systemic shunting
  • Classified as Type A (acute liver failure), B (bypass), C (cirrhosis)
  • Type C subclassified into episodic, persistent, and minimal HE
  • Ammonia is the key neurotoxin but pathogenesis is multifactorial
  • Common precipitants: GI bleeding, infection, electrolyte disorders, high protein, constipation
  • Diagnosis is clinical — excludes other causes of altered mental status
  • Lactulose is first-line therapy; rifaximin is second-line for refractory cases
  • Treatment of underlying precipitant is paramount
  • Liver transplantation is definitive for refractory HE

Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

  • Triad: liver disease, IPVDs, and increased A-a gradient
  • Pulse oximetry <96% on room air is an important screening test
  • Contrast echocardiography is the gold standard for detecting IPVDs
  • Platypnea-orthodeoxia is a characteristic symptom
  • Severity staging based on PaO₂: mild (≥80), moderate (60-79), severe (<60)
  • Liver transplantation is the only definitive treatment
  • Severe HPS (PaO₂ <60 mmHg) qualifies for MELD exception points
  • No effective medical therapy currently exists

Both HE and HPS are important complications of advanced liver disease.
Early recognition, identification of triggers, and appropriate management improve patient outcomes.
