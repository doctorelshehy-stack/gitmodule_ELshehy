HE — Pathology / Pathogenesis

Pathophysiological Sequence: Nitrogenous substances derived from the gut adversely affect brain function. These compounds gain access to the systemic circulation as a result of decreased hepatic function or portal-systemic shunts. Once in brain tissue, they produce alterations of neurotransmission that affect consciousness and behavior, leading to motor dysfunction and extrapyramidal symptoms exhibited in HE.

Key Pathogenic Mechanisms:

Ammonia Hypothesis

Ammonia crosses the blood-brain barrier, is converted to glutamine in astrocytes, causing osmotic swelling and cerebral edema. Impairs postsynaptic inhibition and alters excitatory/inhibitory neurotransmission.

GABAergic Tone

Increased GABAergic tone due to elevated endogenous benzodiazepine receptor ligands. The principal neuro-inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA is increased in the CSF of encephalopathic patients.

Manganese Deposition

Manganese accumulates in the basal ganglia (pallidum) due to decreased biliary excretion, causing parkinsonian-like extrapyramidal symptoms and contributing to neurodegeneration.

Oxidative & Inflammatory

Oxygen free radicals, circulating opioid peptides, and nitric oxide contribute to neuronal dysfunction. Systemic inflammation synergistically worsens ammonia neurotoxicity.

Key Point: The pathogenesis of HE is multifactorial — no single toxin explains all features. Multiple synergistic mechanisms contribute to the neuropsychiatric manifestations.
