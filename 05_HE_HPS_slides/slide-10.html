HE — Clinical Diagnosis & History

The diagnosis of HE is a clinical diagnosis based on symptoms reported by patients and more often their caregivers. There is no single laboratory test that definitively confirms HE — it remains a diagnosis of exclusion after other causes of altered mental status are ruled out.

Key Historical Features:

  • History of confusion and disorientation
  • Lethargy and excessive sleepiness
  • Memory loss and forgetfulness
  • Slowness to respond (bradyphrenia)
  • Personality change with increased aggression
  • Reversal of day and night sleep pattern
  • History of poor compliance with lactulose treatment
  • Overconsumption of proteins in diet
  • Recent GI bleeding or infection
  • Recent diuretic adjustments or dehydration

Key Diagnostic Point: In patients with recurrent admissions for HE, a history of poor compliance with lactulose or overconsumption of proteins is often elicited. In a comatose patient, the presence of clonus may be a sign of HE though it is non-specific.

The diagnosis requires exclusion of other causes of altered mental status including metabolic encephalopathies, intracranial pathology, drug intoxication, and psychiatric disorders.
