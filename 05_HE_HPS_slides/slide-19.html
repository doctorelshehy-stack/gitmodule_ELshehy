HPS — Clinical Diagnosis

History

  • History of underlying chronic liver disease — most symptoms reflect this: fatigue, confusion, abdominal distension, ankle swelling, GI bleeding
  • Shortness of breath (dyspnea) — particularly if it is worsened by standing upright from a recumbent position (platypnea)
  • Orthodeoxia — decrease in arterial oxygen saturation when moving from supine to upright position
  • Exercise intolerance and easy fatigability
  • Progressive dyspnea over months to years

Physical Examination

  • Stigmata of chronic liver disease — jaundice, muscle wasting, gynecomastia, ascites, spider angiomata, leg edema, splenomegaly
  • Typically, the heart and lung examination are normal without evidence of pulmonary edema, pleural effusion or heart failure (these could mimic HPS)
  • Spider nevi — very common in cirrhosis but may be more numerous in HPS
  • Severe ascites can cause elevation of the diaphragm, impair ventilation and lead to tachypnea
  • Central cyanosis — if arterial oxygen saturation is very low, may be noted (bluish discoloration of lips, tongue, and oral mucosa)
  • Digital clubbing (in advanced cases)

Key Clinical Pearl: Platypnea-orthodeoxia (dyspnea and hypoxemia worsened by upright posture) is a classic but not universal feature of HPS. Ask specifically about positional breathlessness in all cirrhotic patients with respiratory symptoms.
