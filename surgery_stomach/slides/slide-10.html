Perforation & Penetration

Perforation
• Common in elderly, M:F = 2:1
• NSAIDs responsible for most
Symptoms
• Sudden-onset, severe, generalized abdominal pain
• Small perforation: epigastrium + RIF pain
• Repeated vomiting → chemical/bacterial peritonitis
Signs
• Shock, tachycardia (pyrexia delayed)
• Board-like rigidity
• Abdomen doesn't move with respiration
Diagnosis
• Erect plain X-ray: free gas under diaphragm (>50%)
• CT imaging more accurate
Treatment
• Resuscitation and analgesia first
• Urgent laparotomy or laparoscopy
• Thorough peritoneal toilet
• Ulcer trimmed with biopsy
• Closed with well-placed sutures
• Omental patch (Graham's patch)
Penetration
• 5-10% of perforating ulcers
• Erode into adjacent organs
• Pancreas most common site
• Diagnosis: severe symptoms + acute complications
• Surgery usually not recommended
— Unless biliary complications present
— Or severe underlying disease
