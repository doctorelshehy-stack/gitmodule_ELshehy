Section 4 • Schistosomiasis

Clinical Features by Pathological Stage

1. Stage of Invasion

  • Cercarial dermatitis (swimmer's itch): Severe itching due to cercarial penetration of skin. Occurs in first infection.
  • Cercarial pneumonitis (eosinophilic pneumonitis): 4-6 weeks, occurs with heavy infection. Patchy lung congestion, eosinophilic sputum, high eosinophilia.

2. Stage of Ova Deposition & Granuloma Formation

Acute Schistosomiasis — "Katayama Syndrome"

  • Common with S. japonicum, followed by S. mansoni, rare with S. haematobium
  • Main problem in immigrants/visitors encountering cercaria for first time
  • After 4-8 weeks of infection: Fever, urticaria, eosinophilia, arthralgia, myalgia, cough & wheezes, diarrhea, abdominal pain, hepatosplenomegaly
  • Diagnosis: Eosinophilia, serological (ELISA), repeated urine and stool analysis

3. Granuloma Formation

S. haematobium:

  • Terminal hematuria
  • Dysuria, frequency & precipitancy
  • Hemospermia (ova in semen if seminal vesicles, vas deferens or prostate affected)

S. japonicum & mansoni:

  • Dysentery & tenesmus
  • Prolapse of anal mucosa
  • Anal fistula & fissures

NB: Dysentery is an inflammatory disorder affecting intestines, resulting in severe diarrhea containing mucus and/or blood, with abdominal pain, tenesmus and may be fever.

4. Stage of Fibrosis — Organ-Specific

Results in Urinary bilharziasis, Intestinal bilharziasis, and Hepatosplenic bilharziasis (detailed in following slides).
