Section 1 • Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

HPS — Definition & Etiology

Definition: HPS is defined by an increased alveolar-arterial gradient on room air with evidence of intrapulmonary vasodilators (IPVDs) occurring in patients with liver disease and in the absence of intrinsic lung disease.

Etiology Key Points

  • The reason why some cirrhotic patients develop HPS and others do not is unclear.
  • The presence of cirrhosis is not essential for the development of HPS — it can occur in noncirrhotic portal hypertension and in ischemic hepatitis (with reversal of intrapulmonary vasodilation with correction of the underlying ischemia).
  • There is no correlation between the degree of arterial hypoxemia or amount of shunting and the severity of liver disease (as measured by the Child-Pugh score).

Clinical Diagnosis — History

  • History of underlying chronic liver disease: fatigue, confusion, abdominal distension, ankle swelling, GI bleeding
  • Shortness of breath, particularly if worsened by standing upright from a recumbent position

Clinical Diagnosis — Physical Exam

  • Stigmata of chronic liver disease
  • Heart and lung exam typically normal (no pulmonary edema, pleural effusion or heart failure)
  • Spider nevi are very common in cirrhosis but may be more numerous in HPS
  • Severe ascites can cause elevation of diaphragm and impair ventilation leading to tachypnea
  • If arterial oxygen saturation is very low, central cyanosis may be noted
