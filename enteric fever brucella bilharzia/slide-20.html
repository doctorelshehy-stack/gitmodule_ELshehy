Section 3 • Brucellosis

Pathogenesis & Clinical Picture

Pathogenesis (3 steps)

  1. Brucella enters body, reaches regional lymph nodes then blood stream (primary bacteremia), then taken by reticuloendothelial system (liver, spleen, lymph nodes, bone marrow). Intracellular location protects from defense mechanisms.
  2. Following multiplication in endoplasmic reticulum, released into blood stream (secondary bacteremia = end of incubation period) with hemolysins and induced cell necrosis.
  3. The cycle repeats with recurrence of bacteremia and fever (undulation) till diagnosis/treatment, death, localization or chronicity. Organism produces inflammation, abscess formation or granuloma formation (delayed hypersensitivity reaction).

Clinical Types (5 types based on duration & localization)

I. Subclinical Brucellosis

Asymptomatic, occurs in high risk group. Serology negative/low positive. Negative blood culture. Negative PCR.

II. Acute (<3mo) & Subacute (3-12mo)

Abrupt/insidious onset. Malaise, headache, weakness, fatigue, anorexia, muscle/joint pain, spinal tenderness (spondylitis). Undulant fever/rigors. Lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, hepatomegaly. Dyspepsia, constipation, diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal pain from hepatic abscesses.

III. Relapsing Brucellosis (~10%)

Recurrence of symptoms. Greater in inadequately treated/untreated patients. Even in properly treated patients due to intracellular location protecting from antibiotics.

IV. Focal (Localized)

May localize in any organ: CNS, CVS, Musculoskeletal, Liver, Spleen, Genitourinary.

V. Chronic (>1 year)

Fever in 50%. Negative blood culture. Serology may be negative.
