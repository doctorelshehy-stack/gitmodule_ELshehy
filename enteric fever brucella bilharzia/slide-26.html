Section 4 • Schistosomiasis

Hepatic Schistosomiasis

Key concept: Hepatic schistosomiasis is a delayed hypersensitivity reaction produced by T-lymphocytes. It is a mesenchymal rather than parenchymal disease: portal tracts are thickened and many tributaries of the portal vein are obstructed → portal hypertension. Formation of granuloma replaced later by fibrous tissue → periportal fibrosis responsible for all sequelae.

Clinical Presentation (5 Stages)

Stage 1: Hepatomegaly

Liver enlarged, tender due to congestion from deposition of embolized ova in portal tracts.

Stage 2: Hepatosplenomegaly

Spleen enlarged (cellular hyperplasia + portal hypertension), liver still enlarged.

Stage 3: Huge Splenomegaly

Liver shrunken due to marked fibrosis, firm, finely nodular, not tender. Spleen hugely enlarged due to portal hypertension.

Stage 4: Ascites

Ascites appears due to both portal hypertension and hypoalbuminemia, added to huge splenomegaly.

Stage 5: Hepatic Cell Failure & Hepatic Coma

Terminal stage due to compression of hepatocytes by fibrosis & ascites + marked reduction of portal blood flow → ischemic affection of hepatocytes.

Complications of Hepatic Schistosomiasis

  1. Huge splenomegaly: Hypersplenism → monocytopenia or pancytopenia. Splenic pain, thrombosis. Easy rupture of spleen. Divarication of recti and abdominal hernias.
  2. Esophageal varices: May rupture → fatal hematemesis
  3. Ascites and its complications: Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) and TB peritonitis
  4. Schistosomal glomerulopathy (nephrotic syndrome): Due to autoimmune reaction resulting in deposition of antigen-antibody complex in glomerular vessels & Bowman's capsule
  5. Chronic salmonellosis
