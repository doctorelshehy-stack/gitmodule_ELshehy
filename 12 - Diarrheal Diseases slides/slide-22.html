Tropical Sprue

Definition & Historical Background

Sprue = Anglicized form of Dutch "sprouw" (1669). Term "tropical sprue" coined in 1880 by Sir Patrick Manson. Characterized by chronic diarrhea, malaise, weight loss, and malabsorption of carbohydrates, fats, vitamin B12, and folate.

Epidemiology

Occurs in tropics within 30° N/S of equator. Common in India, Pakistan, Burma, Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore, Vietnam. Uncommon in Africa, China, Middle East. Affects indigenous populations and visitors staying >1 month. Incidence declining due to improved hygiene, antibiotics, and serologic tests for celiac disease.

Pathogenesis & Causative Organisms

Strong presumption of enteric infection. Supporting facts: (1) Often initiated by acute diarrhea, (2) Epidemic/seasonal epidemiology, (3) Responds to antibiotics. Causative organisms: Bacteria (enterotoxigenic E. coli, K. pneumonia), Protozoa (Cryptosporidium parvum, Isospora belli). No single agent fully proven.

Histology

  • Villus blunting and infiltration of lamina propria (more marked than bacterial overgrowth)
  • Delayed small bowel transit

Treatment & Prognosis

Treatment: Broad-spectrum antibiotic (tetracycline), vitamin B12, and folic acid for 6 months.

Prognosis: Patients improve with treatment; intestinal mucosa reverts toward normal. If no improvement, consider celiac sprue.
