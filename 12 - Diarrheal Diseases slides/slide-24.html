Whipple Disease & Presentation Summary

Whipple Disease

Definition: Multisystemic disease caused by Tropheryma whipplei (gram-positive bacterium). Affects small intestine (malabsorption), joints, CNS, CVS (culture-negative endocarditis).
Epidemiology: Extremely rare, more common in white middle/old-aged males.
Route: Unknown but occupational exposure to animals documented.
Pathogenesis: Macrophages infiltrate lamina propria → disrupted villous function → malabsorption.
Prognosis: Untreated → mortality ~100% after 1 year. Treated for 1 year → good prognosis (70% remission). Relapse in 30-40%.

Investigations & Treatment

Investigations: Sudan stain of stool, serum carotene, 72-h fecal fat, PCR (rpoB gene), serologic tests (shouldn't be used). Histology: Lamina propria full of PAS-positive macrophages. Imaging: CT, small-bowel series, brain MRI for CNS disease.
Treatment: Induction with IV ceftriaxone 2 g once daily OR penicillin G IV for 14 days. Maintenance for 1 year with oral double-strength TMP-SMX twice daily. Alternatives: streptomycin, penicillin V, amoxycillin, chloramphenicol.
