Intestinal Tuberculosis

Causative Organism & Epidemiology

M. tuberculosis (most cases), M. bovis (contaminated dairy products). Accounts for 1-3% of TB worldwide. High risk: HIV infection (most important), malignancies (especially lymphoma), glucocorticoids or anti-TNF agents.

Route, Pathology & Clinical

Route: Direct penetration of intestinal mucosa by swallowed organisms (sputum or contaminated milk/food). Sites: Ileum and cecum most common (75%). Ileocecal valve involvement → incompetence (distinguishes from Crohn). Gross types: Ulcerative (60%), Hypertrophic (10%), Ulcerohypertrophic (30%).

Clinical Presentations

Acute, chronic, or acute-on-chronic (resembles intestinal Crohn's): TB toxemia (night fever, sweating, weight loss), chronic abdominal pain (80-90%), diarrhea/constipation, blood in stool, palpable abdominal mass (25-50%), perianal disease with fistula.

Investigations

Laboratory:

Mild anemia, high ESR/CRP, +ve tuberculin test, +ve Quantiferon Gold, PCR in tissue/ascites

Imaging:

Abd U/S (bowel thickening, lymphadenopathy, ascites), CT (intestinal mass, bowel thickening), Barium study

Biopsy:

Characteristic tuberculoma

Complications & Treatment

Complications:

Intestinal hemorrhage, fistula formation, perforation (uncommon), intestinal obstruction (common — segmental stenotic disease), malabsorption (proximal SIBO)

Treatment:

Anti-tuberculous drugs. Surgical intervention may be required to relieve obstruction.

Barium Study (Imaging Findings)

• Pulled up caecum
• Obtuse ileocecal angle; straightening (Goose neck)
• Steirlin sign: Hurrying of barium due to rapid flow and lack of barium in inflamed site
• Flelschner sign (Inverted umbrella sign): Narrow ileum with thickened ileocecal valve
• Napkin lesions
• Chicken intestine: Hypersegmentation
• Mega Ileum: Dilatation of proximal ileum
